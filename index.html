THE NEW FRONTIER

Reflection
Turbulent Beginnings

The Opening of the AI Era

  • —
    We are no longer waiting for the age of AI to begin. We are living through its opening chapter now.
  • —
    A glance back at just the past few years shows how turbulent and consequential that opening has already been. LLMs emerged and almost immediately began revealing a new scale of possibility — a vast, still-unfolding frontier of capability reshaping how we work, create, and navigate everyday life.
  • —
    The pace of change has been extraordinary. But recent months have made one thing clear: the next phase of the AI era will not be determined by the model alone. It will be determined by the Harness — the system of tools, workflows, and human judgment that shapes how intelligence is actually applied.
PRESENT
The now

Force Multipliers are Already Here

  • —
    The AI future is assembling itself in real time. Agents are starting to support tasks that, until recently, felt deeply human — from research and planning to communication and decision-making.
  • —
    The landscape is evolving along two important tracks. General-purpose agents are becoming useful force multipliers across organizations, helping people move faster across a wide range of tasks. Coding agents, meanwhile, are having a particularly strong impact by automating large parts of the development workflow and enabling individual engineers to work with far greater speed and leverage.
  • —
    Of all the domains AI has touched, none has been transformed more than the one where it first took hold: software development. Coding agents are the most mature, most capable, and most deployed of any AI tools in the wild.
Disruption and opportunity are the same thing, seen from different angles.
VANGUARD
CLAUDE CODE AT THE FRONTIER

From Suggestion to Execution

  • —
    At the current vanguard of coding agents stands Claude Code. Unlike traditional code completion tools that suggest the next line as you type, Claude Code operates at the project level — reading the full codebase, planning across multiple files, executing changes, running tests, and iterating until things work.
  • —
    What started as a developer productivity tool has grown into something broader. At Anthropic, it now powers deep research, video creation, and almost all major agent loops — the coding agent quietly became a general-purpose one.
Getting started is straightforward. Extracting the full potential is not.
OPPORTUNITY
THE ADOPTION GAP

Compounding Advantage Starts Now

  • —
    Today, fewer than a third of developers use coding agents as a regular part of their workflow. Those extracting anywhere near their full potential are a fraction of that.
  • —
    The ones who do are pulling ahead — in their careers, their companies, their side projects. They're shipping faster, operating at a scale that wasn't possible before, and compounding that advantage with every week that passes. For companies, the delta between an AI-augmented team and a traditional one is already becoming hard to ignore.
The gap is real, it is growing, and it does not close on its own. The best time to start is now.

STRUCTURED JOURNEY

Setup • Stack • Orchestration

This guide covers the full Claude Code lifecycle, structured as a progression from first principles to advanced mastery.
  • —
    The guide begins with the fundamentals of coding agents — what they are, how they work, and why the right mental model matters before you run a single command. It then moves into setup: choosing the right subscription, configuring your environment, and pairing Claude Code with the tools that strengthen it most, from IDEs to LM Studio, Obsidian, and more.
  • —
    The core of the guide walks through the full Claude Code stack. Basic usage and project configuration. Memory files and how to structure context that persists. Building and managing skills. Running extended sessions without losing coherence. And at the deeper end — agent orchestration, multi-agent setups, and the architectural decisions that separate a power user from everyone else.
  • —
    Throughout the guide, you will find the kinds of optimizations that rarely make it into official documentation — patterns, habits, and practical lessons that add up over time. Finally, tt ends with hands-on examples that bring everything together, showing not just what each feature does, but how they work together in practice.

There's a quote I once heard: "Cigarettes, coffee, and mathematics at all hours of the day." Supposedly a paraphrase of something Jim Simons once said, or aspired to as a young mathematician. It was his vision of the good life.
I had the fortune to live that life at what I still believe was one of the most magical moments in technological history: the narrow window when the entire world's knowledge was suddenly online, but AI wasn't babysitting us through it.
I learned Analysis from Rudin's books, studied Algebra all the way into abstraction, and met Probability through measures. I wrote my first program in QBasic and my first Python program using penup and pendown commands. I was around to play with AlexNet and Word2Vec when they first started circling through developer communities — back when the whole field was still called Deep Learning. The best part: I’m exactly the right age to be in the thick of AI’s true takeoff.
Along the way I've cycled through degrees, startups, side projects, and a rotating cast of cities. Some worked out, some didn't. Today, I still smoke cigarettes, I drink more coffee than I should, and I'm enjoying all the toys that mathematics has brought us — the things we now call LLMs, Agents, or just AI. And given how fast the space is moving, "at all hours of the day" has never felt more fitting.
